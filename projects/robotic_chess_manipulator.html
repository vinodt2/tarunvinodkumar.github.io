Robotic Chess Manipulator
ME 4405 - Fundamentals of Mechatronics, Georgia Tech · Fall 2025
Team project STM32 · UART · Stepper control Sensing & actuation

This project developed a robotic system capable of grasping and placing chess pieces on a board with accurate positioning. The system combines a 3D-printer gantry, a custom rack-and-pinion grasper, and closed-loop sensing to pick up a specified piece, move it to a new square, and place it reliably within the 3.5 cm by 3.5 cm bounds of each tile.

Motivation & Aim

The goal was to design a generalizable small-object manipulation platform, instantiated as a robotic chess manipulator. A user enters the source and destination squares; the robot moves to the correct position, grasps the piece, translates across the board, and places the piece at the new location.

Performance Metrics
  • Pick and place within the 3.5 cm × 3.5 cm target square bounds.
  • Accurate sensing of when the piece contacts the board to control z-motion.
Mechanical & Mechatronic Design

The custom grasper uses a rack-and-pinion mechanism driven by a 28BYJ stepper motor. The design integrates a rod-bearing interface and opposed paddles to provide a secure, symmetric grasp on standard chess pieces while remaining compact and lightweight.

Sensors & Actuators

Actuators. A 28BYJ stepper motor provides the gripping motion, chosen for its 3.4 N·cm holding torque, low current draw, and small form factor, which satisfied the torque and packaging requirements of the grasper.

Sensors. A Hall-effect sensor detects proximity of the piece to the board, triggering the release and ensuring consistent placement even for pieces of varying height. The sensor's small footprint and sufficient sensitivity allowed simple circuitry and robust operation across multiple piece geometries.

Control & State Machine

The system is orchestrated by an STM microcontroller communicating with a host computer and the 3D-printer gantry. A state machine coordinates motion and grasping: moving to the source square, closing the grasper, lifting, translating to the destination square, sensing board proximity via the Hall-effect sensor, and opening the grasper to release the piece.

Challenges & Iteration
  • Initial motor driver and stepper choice produced jittery, partial-step motion, leading the team to pivot back to lab-standard steppers.
  • An early FSR-based grasp-force sensor proved unreliable and alignment-sensitive, so it was replaced with the more robust Hall-effect sensing scheme.
  • Coordinating communication among the STM, laptop, and 3D-printer controller required careful handling of UART/serial links and debugging tools.
  • Multiple design iterations were needed to tune 3D-printed tolerances and scale the original grasper concept to fit the gantry hardware.
Outcomes & Next Steps

The final system successfully performed repeated grasping, placing, and positioning of chess pieces after extensive iteration. Key technical takeaways included STM-Python communication over UART, step-by-step debugging using LEDs and serial terminals, and the importance of wire management in physical prototypes.

Future work would expand the instrumented board to support arbitrary placement squares by multiplying the Hall-effect sensing scheme, and further optimize the grasper by adding textured contact surfaces and a dedicated feedback loop for grasp quality.
